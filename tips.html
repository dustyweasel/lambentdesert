TIPS:

1) Pegasus / Glacier Bay

Pegasus and Glacier Bay are the same thing these days as far as I can tell. Check both folders.

2) Flush sinks

If the sink has to be a flush cut there's a chance you will find a dxf but then maybe we'll make you bring the sink back anyway. Some of the sink drawings we have suck and only pass inspection because overhang saves the day. There's no guarantee that expanding a sink drawn five years ago by god knows who by 1/4" will match a sink perfectly. Some .dxf's say flush in the filename and those are probably okay.

3) Rectangle, circle, ellipse

If you can't find the .dxf and you still don't want to bring the sink back then is it a rectangle, circle, or ellipse? You could probably just write down the dimensions.

Watch out on elliptical sinks. One out of 500 isn't really an ellipse. Look: ellipse example It's hard to see on that picture but you can usually see it in real life. On an ellipse the curvature of the sink changes continuously. Once in a blue moon the sink is actually 4 arcs. An arc is a piece of a circle where the curvature is constant. (if you imagine any arc continuing it will always wrap around and make a circle) The blue sink on bottom is not an ellipse. It is made out of the four arcs above. The top part of that bottom circle, the left part of that left circle, etc. I can't tell that by looking at the blue sink but in real life I can tell. (Or even if I've been wrong I've never guessed that a sink was an ellipse and had the top come back. If you're pretty sure it's an ellipse then it is. Just watch out for the obvious ones. We never actually cut ellipses, anyway, the machines don't understand them. Whenever you draw an ellipse in CAD the software approximates the shape using arcs just like that picture except it uses more than four arcs.)

The red sink is an ellipse. You might remember in high school we used to draw them by sticking two pins in a board and tying a string to them with slack. Sort of like this only without the math: Ellipse with a string The curvature changes continuously. All we need for those is the two dimensions and tell us whether to shrink for overhang or not.
